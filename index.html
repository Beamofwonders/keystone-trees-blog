Rooted in Life: Why Trees Matter

Trees are more than just carbon sinks. They are pillars of ecosystems, keystone species that structure habitats, sustain biodiversity, and help regulate the climate. Keystone Trees and Mangroves That Shape Ecosystems

  • Oak (Quercus spp.): Supports over 500 species of insects and countless birds and mammals. A keystone in temperate forests.
  • Fig Tree (Ficus spp.): Produces fruit year-round in tropical forests, feeding over 1,200 species â€” from hornbills to monkeys.
  • Acacia (Acacia spp.): Central to savanna food webs; co-evolved with ants and large mammals like giraffes and elephants.
  • Avicennia (Black Mangrove): Anchors coastlines, prevents erosion, and serves as nurseries for fish, crabs, and migratory birds.
  • Rhizophora (Red Mangrove): Forms the backbone of tropical estuarine ecosystems; traps sediment and detoxifies water.

Protecting What Protects Us

Deforestation endangers not just trees, but the web of life they support. Protecting keystone trees and forests is crucial for achieving SDG 13 (Climate Action) and SDG 15 (Life on Land). Reforestation and conservation programs globally must focus on biodiversity value, not just tree count.
